HL Parent Conversation- Real Friends, Real Life
A simple guide for helping kids build in-person friendship
Kids need friends.
Not just people they know. Not just teammates, classmates, or names in a group chat someday. They need real friends they can laugh with, play with, talk to, disagree with, forgive, and grow alongside.
Friendship helps kids learn things they can’t fully learn alone: how to share, take turns, read emotions, solve problems, include others, speak up, and be themselves with someone else.
And in a world full of screens, busy schedules, and packed family calendars, friendship doesn’t always happen automatically. Parents can help make room for it.
Start with the heart
You don’t need to lecture your kid about loneliness or screen time. Keep it simple.
Try saying:
“Friends are a really important part of life. I want to help you have time with people you enjoy—not just at school or practice, but real time to play, talk, and be together.”
For older kids:
“Good friends help you feel known. They help you laugh, work through hard stuff, and remember you’re not alone. I want to help you have more real time with people who are good for you.”
Ask one easy question
Pick a relaxed moment and ask:
“Who do you wish you had more time with?”
That’s it.
Their answer gives you a starting place. Maybe they name a close friend. Maybe they’re not sure. Maybe they need help thinking through who feels safe, fun, or kind.
You can follow up with:
“What do you like about being with them?”
“What’s something simple you’d want to do together?”
Help make it happen
Kids often need help turning friendship into actual plans.
Offer simple options:
“Want to invite them over this weekend?”
“Should we ask if they want to meet at the park?”
“Would it be fun to have them stay for dinner?”
“Do you want help sending the invite?”
For younger kids, this might mean playdates, park time, bike rides, snacks in the backyard, or building something together.
For older kids, it might mean food, a movie, basketball, a walk, studying together, or just giving them space to hang out.
Keep it low-pressure
Friendship does not need to be a big event. In fact, simple is usually better.
A good rhythm might be:
One friend.
 One easy plan.
 A little bit of space.
 Something to eat.
You’re not trying to manage their social life. You’re helping create room for connection.
A simple line to come back to
“Real friendship takes real time.”
That’s the message. Not fear. Not pressure. Not “screens are bad.” Just this: being with people matters.
Try this this week
Ask your kid:
 “Who would be fun to spend time with soon?”
Then help them make one simple plan.
A walk. A park trip. A snack after school. A friend over for an hour. Start small. Real connection grows through ordinary moments.
